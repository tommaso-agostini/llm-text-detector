Obama paid tribute to the victims of the bomb attack, laying a wreath at the Peace Memorial Park and listening to the testimonies of survivors, or hibakusha. His visit is seen as a gesture of reconciliation and a call for global action to eliminate nuclear weapons. In a moving speech delivered at the park's memorial cenotaph, Obama acknowledged the:false promises of the past and the need for a world without nuclear weapons. "We have known the agony of violence in our own time," he said, "indifferent to the beauty of our writings; hostile to the God we worship; tolerant of a funded hatred thatutz the world over claims a justified God." He also highlighted the irony of our time: "In the 70 years since nuclear weapons were first deployed, we've seen them proliferate to more than two dozen countries; and the odds of a nuclear attack have increased in recent history according to many experts." Obama's visit comes at a time when nuclear tensions are rising globally, with North Korea's recent nuclear tests and the ongoing geopolitical tensions with nations like Iran and Russia. His appeal for a world free from nuclear weapons is not only a moral imperative but also a strategic necessity in an increasingly fragile and unpredictable international landscape. The U.S. president also expressed his commitment to working towards this goal, acknowledging that it won't be easy and will require cooperation and diplomacy from all nations. The Japanese government welcomed Obama's visit, seeing it as a significant step towards healing old wounds and fostering future cooperation. Prime Minister Shinzo Abe accompanied Obama during his visit and expressed hope that the U.S. president's actions would inspire other world leaders to work towards nuclear disarmament.
